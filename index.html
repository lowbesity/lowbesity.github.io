Vision


Proactively dealing with a major health crisis

One of the main challenges of population health in today’s western world is the continuing increase of obesity. In the United States, more than 2 in 3 adults are overweight or obese and as many as 1 in 6 children and adolescents are considered obese. The numbers are horrifying - the consequences of obesity can be very severe and include development of diabetes, cardiovascular disease and the development of cancer. Various ways to combat the problem of a rising obese population are already implemented but the most efficient ones (usually involving surgical reduction of the stomach) are both invasive and expensive.

We believe that there should be an easier solution for people struggling with being overweight and a strategy which deals with both treatment and prevention. The aim of this project therefore is to produce a supplement (in the form of a pill) which contains bacterial species that naturally reside in the gut of humans. These bacteria are known to produce several molecules which aid in the fat metabolism of humans. Our goal is to bring an alternative solution to the problem, provide an easily accessible way of being proactive and encourage the public to embrace the power of their bodies and trust their gut!

A Healthy Biome for a Healthy Human

Product


A product with a long term focus

Recent years have seen a massive increase in the research into the human microbiome. The composition of our individual biome has been shown to have a large impact on our physiology - on the way our body functions.

Although microbiome research is a very complex area of study, a clear difference between the biome of lean and obese individuals has been found, with a link between microbiome composition and incidence of obesity identified.

Specifically, molecular targets produced by bacteria in our gut have been found to regulate the metabolism of our body. These molecules cause a systemic* increase in fatty acid oxidation (meaning our cells burn more fat) while reducing the fatty acid synthesis (production of fat molecules).

In the LowBe supplement we have incorporated uniquely designed bacteria which produce these molecules. Our supplement contains several different strains, ensuring the full spectra of known beneficial molecules are included.

This supplement will provide a more easily accessible way for the general public to treat and prevent their overweight. With LowBe we aspire to create a world where people embrace their good bacteria.

* systemic refers to something which affects the whole body rather than a single organ, i.e. something that acts throughout our system.

Embracing The Power of Your Microbiome

Science


The Role of Short Chain Fatty Acids (SCFA) in Systemic Regulation of Human Metabolism

The Brief Version

​

Short Chain Fatty Acids (SCFA) can be produced by microbes in our gut and subsequently be taken up into the serum. Depending on the relative concentration of these molecules several changes occur in the metabolic regulation of various cell types throughout our body. The mechanism of this regulation varies between different types of tissue, however the physiological outcome is essentially the same; there is an upregulation of fatty acid oxidation and a simultaneous downregulation of fatty acid synthesis. Clearly, this means there is a systematic change in how the body deals with and retains fat.

SCFAs have been shown to result in several beneficial changes events – in addition to the effect on body energy homeostasis they also seem to increase insulin sensitivity and contribute to improved glucose homeostasis.

The Detailed Version

Short Chain Fatty Acids (SCFA) are saturated, aliphatic organic acids ranging from 1-6 carbons in length. In the human gut the SCFAs acetate (C2), propionate (C3) and butyrate (C4) are the most abundant ones. These molecules are the resulting end product of fermentation of indigestible foods (mainly indigestible carbohydrates such as plant cell-wall polysaccharides and resistant starches, referred to as dietary fibers) by commensal microbacteria in the gut. Majority of the SCFA production occurs by anaerobic bacteria in the caecum and the proximal colon.

Multiple studies, both observational and interventional, have been conducted around the effects of SCFAs. The evidence provided points to the numerous beneficial effects of the molecules in regards to control of body weight. SCFAs have been shown to impact host metabolism through several mechanisms, both by increasing energy expenditure and stimulating release of e.g. anorexic hormones. The Bacterial Species Under Consideration

It is generally accepted that one of the key differences between the microbiota of lean individuals and overweight or obese individuals is the presence and relative abundance of commensal bacteria producing SCFAs.

We recommend that two main species are included in the LowBe supplement; Faecalibacterium prausnitzii and species of the Bifidobacterium genus.

BifidobacteriumBifidobacteria obtain carbon and energy through fermentation of host and dietary carbohydrates. The products of their fermentation include butyrate and acetate. In addition to producing SCFAs, Bifidobacteria have been indicated to exert multiple protective intestinal actions including prevention of various gastrointestinal disorders (such as IBS) and resistance to pathogens through production of antimicrobials. In a meta-analysis of over 60 individual studies on effect of pre- and probiotics done in 2013 the authors found that an increase in Bifidobacteria was related to the main effect of associated weight loss.
FaecalibacteriumF.prausnitzii is one of the most abundant species in the human gut but, more importantly, it is the most abundant butyrate-producing bacteria of the gastrointestinal tract. A link has been established between changes in abundance of F.prausnitzii and dysbiosis in several human disorders, leading researchers to evaluate the bacteria's use as a sensor of health. This microbe is therefore recommended for the LowBe supplement due to its high output of SCFAs combined with other indicated health benefits.
The Genetic Engineering

To obtain maximal efficiency from the bacteria in the LowBe supplement, genetically engineered strains will be created. These will include constitutive promoters placed upstream of the genes encoding key enzymes of the fermentation pathway leading to SCFA production. Thus, the production of SCFAs will only be limited by substrate availability.


It is also suggested that an R&D investigation is conducted into the potential in modifying promoter regions or completely eliminating alternative pathways to further increase SCFA output. Certain parts of alternative pathways which normally lead to a variety of metabolic waste products (e.g. those using amino acids as substrate) could be ‘redirected’ to predominantly produce SCFAs. Other alternative metabolic pathways natural to the organism could also be completely eliminated in favour of those producing the desired SCFAs.

A Global Problem, A Global Market

Business Model


Tapping Into a New Era of Therapeutics

The worldwide rate of obesity has nearly doubled since 1980 and recent data (2011) shows that just over 200 million adult men and just under 300 million adult women are obese. A large-scale study published in 2016 showed additional large increase in obesity, severe obesity and morbid obesity in both men and women worldwide. It was concluded that if post-2000 trends continue like this, as is indicated by all data, the probability of reaching the global obesity target is essentially zero. Forecasts predict that the combined prevalence of obesity and severe obesity will soon be about 24% for men and over 30% in women globally. The obesity therapy market is expected to reach a value of $8.4 billion by 2022 and the combined market of size for diabetes and obesity drugs is estimated to reach $162.3 billion (according to GlobalData Inc. and ResearchMoz Global).

We suggest our company taps into this large and growing market segment, and we have a product proposal which would enable us to take a unique position. Our therapeutic solution would be an efficient, non-invasive alternative treatment for this widespread condition and provide not only a treatment but also serve as a preventative therapeutics model to cope with obesity. There is additionally great potential for a version of the product to be sold as an over-the-counter (OTC) drug alternative for dieting, giving us access to both the commercial and clinical market for weight loss and obesity treatment.

We predict little to no issues with approval from the FDA and similar agencies because of the biocontainment strategy incorporated into the genetic engineering of our product solution (see subsection ‘genetic engineering’).

After the initial creation of engineered bacterial strains the production would only involve maintenance and growth of the bacteria and none of the extraction and purification steps typical for biological therapeutics. The absence of these steps would thus make the manufacturing of the LowBe supplement pill relatively cheap, giving us a larger gross profit margin than expected for other therapeutics. Additionally, the use of our already established industrial scale production chains would allow for fast development, production and distribution of the pills.

Finally, targeting the fermentation pathway involving SCFA through the use of genetically engineered bacterium could produce a window of opportunity for patenting - thus securing a section of this market which, according to future projections, will only continue to increase.

It does not have to stop here

It is now widely accepted in the medical and research community that the gut microbiota plays a large role in shaping human physiology. Even though more studies have to be conducted to completely understand the full impact of the microbiome on human physiology, many researchers agree that the microbiota presents a major target area for new therapeutic strategies for both disease prevention and treatment.

Exploiting the effects of SCFA is just one of these potential strategies. The molecular mechanisms of SCFA in relation to human metabolism is an active area of research and multiple studies point to the various beneficial effects of these. The SCFA molecules have been highlighted for their potential use as prevention or treatments for a further range of human afflictions e.g. the metabolic syndrome, bowel disorder and even certain types of cancer. Development of the proposed product and genetically engineered bacteria would put us at the forefront of this emerging area segment. Further R&D, building on our initial product, could allow us to seize a big part of both the market for metabolic health and the general use of microbes in methods of prevention and treatment in clinical medicine.
